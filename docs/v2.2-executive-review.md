# Promptly Score v2.2 — Executive Review

**For:** Charlie Raybould **· Prepared:** June 2026 **· Status:** DRAFT — decision document
**· Purpose:** the final decision before any live-implementation discussion. Source of truth:
`~/Documents/methodology`. Nothing has been applied to the 242 live tools.

## 1. What changed from v2.1 → v2.2

| Area | v2.1 (current live) | v2.2 | 
|---|---|---|
| **Pillar weights** | Roughly even, Privacy-led (Privacy 25 · Safeguarding/Age/Transparency 20 · Accessibility 15) | **Safeguarding 35 · Privacy 25 · Age 15 · Accessibility 15 · Transparency 10** — the two "legal-barrier" pillars now carry **60%** |
| **Floor → cap** | Hard floor (flat "max 6.9 if safeguarding low") — a cliff | **Graduated cap:** composite ≤ min(Safeguarding, Privacy) + 1.0 — smooth, no cliffs |
| **Avoid trigger** | Avoid below 4.0 | **Avoid if Safeguarding *or* Privacy < 4.5** (4.0–4.4 now flips to Avoid) |
| **Evidence basis** | Not formalised | **Review Basis Tiers** — Desk / Hands-On / Classroom, shown on every score; **desk caps Safeguarding & Privacy at 8.5** |
| **Rigour** | Lighter | 0.5-step pillar scoring; **cited evidence per pillar**; blind pillar scoring; mandatory 75-word rationale; **named reviewer + date + version + basis**; second-reader on Avoid and band-edge; calibration set + provisional tolerances; separation of duties |

The verdict bands are unchanged (9.0–10 Promptly Recommended · 8.0–8.9 Strong · 7.0–7.9 Worth
Considering · 6.0–6.9 Proceed Carefully · <6.0 Avoid). v2.2.1 adds one clarification: how the
universal Safeguarding pillar is read for **staff-only systems** (CPOMS etc.).

## 2. Why it changed
- **Defensibility.** The old hard floor created a cliff — a 0.1 input swinging the output two
  points. That is the first thing a DPO's or vendor's solicitor circles. The graduated cap rises
  smoothly and survives scrutiny.
- **Risk to the child, not what flatters us.** Safeguarding and Data Privacy are *legal barriers*
  to lawful adoption in a UK school, not quality questions — so they should dominate. (Honest
  tension: Transparency is the brand's identity but only its fourth-ranked risk; the methodology
  reflects risk, not vanity.)
- **Reproducibility = the moat.** Cited evidence, basis tiers, blind scoring and calibration make
  "every score is reproducible" actually true — the part competitors cannot copy without becoming
  us. A policy read cannot earn the confidence a 9–10 asserts; hence the desk ceiling.

## 3. Impact on existing scores
From calibration batch 1 (the only tools scored under v2.2 — drafts, CR sign-off pending):

| Tool | Basis | Live (v2.1) | v2.2 | Δ |
|---|---|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| MagicSchool.ai | Hands-On | 9.2 | 9.1 | −0.1 |
| Google Classroom | Desk | 9.7 | 8.0 | −1.7 |
| ClassDojo | Desk | 9.4 | 7.9 | −1.5 |
| CPOMS | Desk | 9.3 | 7.5 | −1.8 |
| Quizizz / Wayground | Desk | 8.5 | 7.6 | −0.9 |

**Every score fell. The one hands-on tool barely moved (−0.1); the four desk-reviewed tools fell
0.9–1.8.** The desk-review 8.5 ceiling on the 60%-weighted legal-barrier pillars is the dominant
cause. Extrapolated to the catalogue: **most of the 242 will drop ~1–2 points and re-band**, and
the current 9-heavy distribution compresses toward 7–8 for any desk-only tool.

*Caveat:* the live scores are **synthetic placeholders** (`derivePillars`), so today's "delta" is
partly an artefact of comparing real-ish v2.2 desk scores against fabricated baselines. The true
picture only exists once tools are genuinely re-reviewed.

## 4. Impact on people

**Reviewers.** A much heavier process: evidence cited per pillar, basis recorded, blind scoring,
75-word rationale, second-reader on every Avoid and band-edge case, calibration onboarding +
quarterly re-scoring, conflict checks. It needs a **named human reviewer (CR)** who *owns* each
score — AI can draft, a human must sign. This is a throughput bottleneck: 242 tools at this rigour
is a sustained programme, not a sprint, and cannot be done by AI alone.

**Schools.** More honest, defensible verdicts they can take to SLT, governors and the DPO — and
the basis tier tells them *how* a score was earned (desk vs tested). But many familiar tools will
read lower — "Worth Considering" where they said "Recommended" — which could unsettle schools
mid-transition without clear communication that the methodology was tightened, not the tools
worsened.

**Vendors.** Many will see downgrades and push back. The defences are built in: the public Score
Integrity Statement, the public change record, the named reviewer, and separation of duties
(Oliver cannot score his own vendors). Crucially, v2.2 gives vendors a **constructive route up** —
hands-on/classroom evidence and closing documentary gaps (DPA, VPAT, model disclosure) raise the
score legitimately. Avoid verdicts still publish (tagged by cause) rather than being hidden.

## 5. Risks
1. **Mass re-banding** of live scores → vendor backlash, "downgrade" screenshots, and a change-
   stamp on nearly every score (≥0.3 moves). Needs a migration/comms plan.
2. **Capacity.** Real re-review (evidence + reviewer + second reader) for 242 tools is a large,
   slow, human-dependent effort.
3. **Desk-only = mediocre-looking.** Without hands-on testing, most of the catalogue caps at
   "Worth Considering." Reaching "Recommended" costs reviewer time per tool.
4. **Premature publication.** Tolerances (±0.5 pillar / ±0.3 composite) are still **provisional**;
   publishing before they're validated undermines the reproducibility claim that *is* the product.
5. **Low-band calibration gap.** Reviewers have no anchored references for failing tools yet
   (Avoid-tier anchors must be sourced deliberately).

## 6. Benefits
1. **Genuinely defensible** to a DPO, governor or solicitor — no cliffs, named evidence,
   reproducible working, basis stated.
2. **Safeguarding-first**, matching the KCSIE positioning and the real risk to children.
3. **The moat** — rubric + cited evidence + calibration is the thing competitors can't copy.
4. **Trust through transparency** — public methodology, named reviewers, public change history,
   failures shown not hidden; "no score is for sale" becomes demonstrable.
5. A **constructive incentive** for vendors to improve (and be re-reviewed) rather than to lobby.

## 7. Recommendation & decisions for CR
**Adopt v2.2 (with the v2.2.1 staff-system clarification) as the standard — but do not flip live
scores.** The methodology is sound and defensible; the danger is operational, not conceptual.

Recommended path before any live change:
1. **Validate the calibration tolerances** (≥3 reviewers double-score the calibration set; set the
   tolerance at the observed spread). Until this passes, nothing publishes.
2. **Complete the calibration set** (cap-binding, both Avoid floors, N/A, desk-vs-hands-on) and the
   **low-band anchors** — including deliberately sourcing failing tools.
3. **Phase the re-review**, hands-on, starting with the most-visible tools (the Homepage set) —
   they need hands-on evidence to stay in the top band.
4. **Plan the migration/comms** for the inevitable re-banding *before* any score changes publicly.
5. Keep everything **docs-only** until 1–2 are done.

hands-on, tolerance-validated rollout (no live flip until validated)? (c) confirm CR as named
