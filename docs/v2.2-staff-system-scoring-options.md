# v2.2 — Staff-System Scoring Options (the CPOMS problem)

**Decision paper. No tools scored.** A methodology ruling is requested before any
staff-facing system is scored under v2.2. Source of truth: `~/Documents/methodology`.

## The problem
The **Safeguarding** pillar (35%) is defined in §05B as *child-harm exposure* — "whether
the tool can expose any child to harm, across content, contact, conduct and commerce."
The rubric bands assume a **pupil-facing** product.

But a large class of catalogue tools have **no pupil-facing surface at all** — staff only.
And §05A is explicit: **Safeguarding and Data Privacy can never be N/A.** So we cannot
skip the pillar, yet its child-exposure definition doesn't fit. CPOMS exposed this in
calibration batch 1 (scored Safeguarding 8.0 essentially because *no child can reach harm
through a staff records system* — a near-vacuous high score carrying 35% of the composite).

## Two sub-classes (they are not the same)
**Class A — Admin / MIS / analytics (no safeguarding function).** Staff-only; pupils never
log in. Safeguarding-by-child-exposure is near-vacuous.
*Examples in catalogue:* SIMS (Capita), Bromcom, Arbor MIS, ScholarPack, Pupil Asset,
Satchel One, FFT Aspire, SISRA Analytics, 4Matrix, Compass Education, Schoolytics.

**Class B — Safeguarding / monitoring systems (safeguarding IS the product).** Staff-facing,
but their entire purpose is child protection (recording concerns, filtering/monitoring).
Here a Safeguarding score *should* mean something — the **efficacy and integrity** of the
safeguarding function — not "can a child reach harm."
*Examples:* CPOMS, MyConcern, Smoothwall Monitor, Impero Education Pro, Securus, Lightspeed.

Any ruling must handle **both** — they currently collide under one pillar definition.

## Options

### Option 1 — Literal exposure (no change)
Score Safeguarding strictly on child-harm exposure. Staff-only tool → no pathway → high
score (desk-capped 8.5).
- **Pro:** faithful to the §05B boundary; no methodology change.
- **Con:** misleading — an 8/10 "Safeguarding" on a records system reads as *we assessed its
  safeguarding quality* (we didn't); inflates the composite via the 35% weight on a vacuous
  high; makes Class A tools look artificially strong. **This is the status quo and the reason
  CPOMS landed 7.5 partly on a hollow 8.0.**

### Option 2 — Re-scope Safeguarding for staff systems to *data/process integrity*
For staff-only tools, Safeguarding measures **the security and integrity of the child data the
tool holds and the safeguarding processes it supports** (breach of safeguarding records = real
child harm), with its own band descriptors.
- **Pro:** meaningful and honest; captures the actual risk; works well for Class B.
- **Con:** heavy overlap with **Data Privacy** → double-counting (a strong security posture
  scores twice); blurs the §05B exclusive-boundary rule (the methodology's moat).

### Option 3 — A documented **Staff-System sub-rubric** (recommended direction)
Add a classified tool type "Staff System (non-pupil-facing)" with explicit, separate band
descriptors for Safeguarding, split by sub-class:
- **Class A (admin/MIS):** Safeguarding scored on *governance fit + child-data protection
  posture + access control/audit* — NOT child exposure. Lower ceiling than a pupil-facing
  top band, so it can't inflate. A mandatory disclosure states "staff-only; no pupil-exposure
  assessment applies."
- **Class B (safeguarding/monitoring):** Safeguarding scored on *efficacy of the safeguarding
  function* (does it reliably capture/escalate concerns; filtering/monitoring effectiveness;
  KCSIE/Keeping-Children-Safe-Online alignment) — a high-value, defensible assessment.
- Keep the exclusive boundary: *data residency/retention/DPA* stays in Privacy; *records
  security as it bears on child safety* sits in Safeguarding with a one-line cross-reference
  to avoid double-counting.
- **Pro:** honest, tailored, preserves "every score means something"; no vacuous highs;
  Class B finally gets a meaningful safeguarding verdict.
- **Con:** methodology complexity; needs new band text + a tool-type classifier; small drift
  risk → mitigated by anchor exemplars (see the anchor proposal).

### Option 4 — Governance amendment: allow Safeguarding N/A for Class A only
Amend §05A to permit Safeguarding = N/A **solely** for staff-only, non-pupil-facing admin
tools, with rescaled weights (Privacy becomes the dominant legal barrier).
- **Pro:** removes the vacuous-high distortion cleanly; simple to compute.
- **Con:** breaks a stated **invariant** ("Safeguarding can never be N/A") — a core brand
  promise; requires a version bump and public note; risks "they switch off safeguarding when
  it suits them" criticism. **Not recommended** for that reputational reason.

### Option 5 — Cap the Safeguarding contribution for staff systems
Keep Safeguarding non-N/A but, for staff systems, cap its *score contribution* (e.g. it cannot
exceed the Privacy score) so a hollow high can't lift the composite.
- **Pro:** minimal text change; neutralises the inflation.
- **Con:** opaque/ad-hoc; harder to explain to a DPO than a clear sub-rubric.

---

## Recommendation
**Option 3** (documented Staff-System sub-rubric, split A/B), with the Class A disclosure and
the explicit Safeguarding↔Privacy boundary note. It is the only option that keeps every score
meaningful, avoids inflating composites, and gives Class B safeguarding tools the assessment
they deserve — without breaking the "never N/A" invariant. It does cost a §05B addition and a
version bump (v2.2 → v2.2.1), and needs anchor exemplars to hold the line.

## What a ruling unblocks
- **CPOMS** (Class B) — re-score Safeguarding on safeguarding-function efficacy, not exposure.
- **Satchel One** (Class A) — currently blocked in the readiness report pending this ruling.
- ~15 further catalogue MIS/monitoring tools that will hit the same wall.
